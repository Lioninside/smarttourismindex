Smart Tourism Index

A data-based ranking of Swiss places as travel bases

Smart Tourism Index treats destinations as bases for exploring Switzerland — not just as endpoints. The question is not how well-known a place is. It is how well it works locally (Base Score) and what it connects to beyond itself (Access Score).

The index draws on a broad set of public data covering local quality, landscape, culture, reach, and tourism pressure. It is one structured way to compare places across consistent, measurable criteria.

The goal is to identify strong travel bases — including places that may deserve more attention in a tourism system shaped by concentrated demand.

The Score

Two layers. One ranking.

Base Score measures how well a place works for staying, getting around, and spending time there. It incorporates local quality signals, landscape, culture, and tourism pressure.

Access Score measures what a place connects to beyond itself — major destinations, natural features, and experiences reachable within a reasonable travel radius.

A destination can rank well for one reason or both. A well-functioning local base. Strong regional reach. The most interesting places in the index tend to combine both.

The score is data-based and built around one defined question: how strong is this place as a base for exploration?

What It Is

A different lens on destinations

Most travel platforms are optimised around attention, reviews, or pricing. That is what they are built for.

Smart Tourism Index asks a different question. It looks at how useful a place is as a base and how well it connects to the surrounding region. The ranking is built from public data — not editorial judgment or user ratings.

This does not replace platforms like Google Maps, TripAdvisor, or booking sites. It adds a different perspective: one focused on reach, local quality, and visitor pressure.

This is not a booking tool. It is not a review platform. It is not a verdict on whether a place is worth visiting.

How It Was Built

Public data. Careful integration.

The hard part was not finding the data. It was making datasets built for different purposes, systems, and geographies work together in a way that produces fair, comparable results.

Data sources include the Swiss Federal Statistical Office BFS, MeteoSwiss, Swiss GTFS, swissTLM3D, OpenStreetMap, and more.

Cleaning, aligning, and calibrating those sources was most of the work. A ranking is only as credible as the decisions made about how its inputs relate to each other.

How To Read It

Why places score differently

A place does not rank high because it is well known.

It ranks high when it works well as a base, has lower visitor pressure, and opens up strong access to the wider region.

That is why some famous destinations score lower than expected. High tourism pressure is factored in and can reduce a score even for well-known places. A smaller, less obvious place can rank above it if it offers better local conditions and comparable reach.

The ranking is designed to surface a different set of answers to the question of where to base a trip.

Public data is available. Comparable data is harder.

About the Project

An independent project by Clemens Bartlome

Smart Tourism Index started as a side project outside my day job. The question behind it was simple: can public data produce a more neutral, structured view of destination value?

My background is in Swiss tourism, strategy, international sales, and business transformation — including roles at Graubünden Ferien and Switzerland Travel Centre. This project comes out of that experience.

The index is open source and independent. It is not connected to any current professional role or organisation.
