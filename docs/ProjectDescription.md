This concept shifts the perspective from evaluating destinations based on their standalone prominence to assessing their **functional value as a base location**. Instead of asking how well-known or attractive a place is on its own, the model evaluates **how much can be accessed from that place within a defined travel time**. The underlying idea is to identify locations that may not be widely recognized but provide **high strategic value due to their connectivity and surrounding opportunities**.

## Core Concept

At the center of the approach is the construction of a **travel-time-based catchment model**. For each potential base location, a realistic **1-hour travel envelope** is calculated using actual mobility conditions rather than straight-line distance. Within this accessible area, the model evaluates the **quantity, diversity, and relevance of reachable experiences**.

This transforms the evaluation logic from a static, place-based ranking into a **relational accessibility model**. A location is not primarily judged by what it contains, but by **what it enables**.

The key outcome is the identification of **high-leverage base locations**: places that offer disproportionately rich access to surrounding assets relative to their level of recognition or demand.

## Conceptual Logic

The model is built on two interacting dimensions:

### 1. Access Value

This represents the **aggregate opportunity space** reachable within the defined travel time. It captures both:

* **Quantity**: how many options are available
* **Diversity**: how varied those options are

Typical components include, for example:

* Access to lakes and waterfront areas
* Access to outdoor and natural environments such as hiking networks or landscapes
* Access to cultural assets such as museums or heritage sites
* Presence of dining and hospitality infrastructure

The emphasis is not on isolated features, but on the **combined experiential range** that can be activated from a single base.

### 2. Relative Prominence

This dimension reflects how established or widely used a location already is within tourism flows. Indicators such as overnight stays or accommodation intensity can serve as proxies for this.

The purpose is not to penalize well-known destinations, but to provide context. A location with high access value but lower prominence may represent **untapped potential**, while a highly prominent location may already be fully recognized and heavily frequented.

## Resulting Insight: Identification of High-Potential Base Locations

By combining these dimensions, the model surfaces locations that exhibit:

* **High access value**
* **Lower-than-expected prominence**

These locations function as **efficient base camps**: they enable a wide range of experiences with relatively low friction, while often avoiding the pressure associated with highly saturated destinations.

The concept therefore reframes the idea of a “hidden” destination. It is not defined purely by obscurity, but by a **mismatch between accessibility potential and current recognition**.

## Overtourism Perspective

An important extension of this concept is its relevance for addressing **overtourism**. One of the structural challenges in tourism is that demand often concentrates on a relatively small number of highly visible destinations. This concentration is frequently reinforced by broad popularity signals such as media exposure, social media visibility, recommendation loops, or influencer-driven attention.

A data-driven base-location model offers a more **systematic and forward-looking alternative**. Instead of relying primarily on visibility or perception, it evaluates destinations according to their **measurable capacity to distribute visitor flows across a wider opportunity space**.

This makes the approach particularly valuable in three ways:

### 1. Smarter distribution of demand

The model can identify places that are well-positioned to absorb and distribute visitor activity without depending on the already most saturated hotspots. In this way, tourism demand can be guided toward locations with strong accessibility and experience value, but lower pressure.

### 2. Decision-making based on structure rather than hype

This approach does not replace inspiration, emotional appeal, or storytelling, all of which remain important in travel decisions. However, it adds a **modern, data-driven layer of intelligence** to destination evaluation. It helps shift part of the decision process from broad popularity signals toward **objective accessibility and opportunity analysis**.

### 3. High impact through better planning

Because the model focuses on where visitors can stay and what they can access from there, it supports a more efficient use of regional tourism systems. This can contribute to:

* better spatial distribution of guests
* reduced pressure on heavily visited destinations
* stronger visibility for overlooked but well-connected places
* a more balanced use of existing infrastructure and transport networks

In this sense, the model is not only a destination-ranking tool. It can also function as a **practical planning instrument** for smarter tourism management.

## Methodological Foundation

A critical element of the concept is the use of **travel-time isochrones** instead of geometric distance. This ensures that the accessibility model reflects real-world conditions:

* Transport networks and schedules
* Topography and infrastructure constraints
* Modal differences, for example rail versus road access

This is particularly important in geographically complex environments, where equal distances can result in very different travel times.

## Structural Model

At a high level, the model can be expressed as:

* a **base accessibility score**, derived from the weighted combination of reachable assets within the time envelope
* a **contextual adjustment**, reflecting the relative prominence of the location

The interaction of these elements produces an index that highlights locations offering **high optionality with relatively low saturation**.

## Positioning of the Concept

This approach differs fundamentally from traditional destination rankings. Rather than answering *where to go*, it addresses a more operational question:

**Where to stay in order to maximize flexibility, variety, and efficiency during a trip?**

This makes the concept particularly aligned with the behavior of independent travelers, who often prioritize:

* ease of day trips
* variety of accessible experiences
* reduced logistical complexity

## Key Strength

The strength of the model lies in its focus on **access leverage**. It identifies locations where a single decision, namely choosing a base, unlocks a broad set of opportunities and can improve the overall efficiency and richness of a trip.

At the same time, this logic has relevance beyond the individual traveler. It supports a more balanced and evidence-based tourism system by showing that destination value does not need to be concentrated only in the most visible places.

## Critical Considerations

Several methodological challenges need to be managed carefully:

* **Quality vs. quantity**: Pure counts of accessible points may overestimate value if not balanced by relevance or diversity
* **Balanced treatment of prominence**: Highly popular destinations can still offer strong access value and should not be undervalued
* **Comparability of place types**: Different categories of locations, such as urban centers and small towns, may require segmentation to ensure meaningful comparisons

## Conceptual Framing

The most accurate way to describe this model is as a:

**Base-location accessibility index**

It evaluates destinations not as endpoints, but as **platforms for exploration**, shifting the focus from attraction-based ranking to **network-based travel value**.
